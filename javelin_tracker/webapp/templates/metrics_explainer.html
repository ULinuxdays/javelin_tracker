How we calculate load & readiness

Plain-language formulas so you can trust the dashboard, analytics, and reports.

Why it matters

We track training stress so you can spot spikes, balance intensity, and reduce injury risk while chasing PBs.

Units & windows

  • Distance — meters (m)
  • Load — arbitrary units (AU) = RPE × duration (minutes)
  • RPE — 1–10 perceived effort
  • Windows — acute = last 7 days (average), chronic = last 28 days (average)

Session load

What a single session contributes.

Formula: Session load = RPE × duration (minutes).

Example: 45 min × RPE 7 = 315 AU (arbitrary units). Throws/implements still matter, but RPE × time captures internal load consistently.

We also store event, notes, tags, and implement weight to keep context.

Weekly / block load

Averages and variability that feed ACWR and strain.

  • Acute load (7d mean): Average daily load across the last 7 days.
  • Chronic load (28d mean): Average daily load across the last 28 days.
  • Monotony: 7-day mean ÷ 7-day standard deviation (flagging repetitive, non-varied training).
  • Strain: 7-day load sum × monotony — highlights heavy weeks with little variation.
  • Blocks: If you define blocks, we still surface the most recent acute/chronic/monotony signals.

ACWR (acute:chronic workload ratio)

Flags sudden spikes by comparing short-term to longer-term load.

Formula: ACWR = Acute 7-day average ÷ Chronic 28-day average.

Typical guidance: keep ACWR near 0.8–1.3 to avoid abrupt spikes. We also bump risk if monotony is high or weekly strain is excessive.

Readiness signals

How we surface readiness and risk cues.

  • We look at load trends, ACWR, monotony/strain, and RPE averages in your selected window.
  • “Risk” tags appear when acute load exceeds the sweet spot or when monotony/strain stay high without variation.
  • Readiness multipliers are driven by trend slopes, percent changes, and effect sizes (recent vs baseline).
  • Use tags/notes to capture context (surface, wind, technical focus) that informs decisions.
These metrics are guidance to support coaching decisions and should complement your observation and medical input.
